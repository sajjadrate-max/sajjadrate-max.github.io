A Complete Introduction to Abjad Numerals

Each of the 28 letters of the Arabic alphabet has been assigned a specific numeric value, known as the "Abjad" system. This system has been in use across the Islamic world for centuries and holds a foundational place in traditional sciences, particularly Ilm-e-Jafar.

The Order of Abjad

The Abjad letters are traditionally grouped into eight sets: Abjad, Hawwaz, Hutti, Kalaman, Sa'fas, Qarashat, Thakhaz, and Dazagh. Each group's letters carry their own numeric value — for example, Alif equals 1, Ba equals 2, Jeem equals 3, Dal equals 4, and so on up to Ya at 10. From there, values continue in tens (Kaf through Sad) and then in hundreds (Qaf through Ghayn).

Traditional Uses

In earlier times, the Abjad system was also used for chronogramming — encoding the year of a significant event within a phrase, known as a "historical chronogram" (tareekhi mada). Beyond this, spiritual sciences use the Abjad values of names and questions to derive numerical analyses.

Importance in Ilm-e-Jafar

Both branches of Ilm-e-Jafar — Akhbar and Asar — rely fundamentally on Abjad numerals. The numeric values of the letters in a question, name, or date are summed and processed according to specific traditional rules to arrive at a result. This is a centuries-old traditional method used for guidance, not a claim to definitive scientific fact.
